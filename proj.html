What is Tobacco?

Tobacco is a plant whose leaves are fermented for a variety of uses. The leaves contain nicotine and many harmful chemicals. Nicotine is used in a number of different drugs and is highly addictive.

How is it administered?

Tobacco is typically smoked (cigarettes, cigars, pipes) that deliver it to the brain very quickly. There are also e-cigarettes, snuff, and chewing tobacco. Nicotine is also sometimes used in the form of inhalers and gum to help people quit.

How does it affect the body?

Beyond the direct impacts of nicotine, tobacco also includes many harmful chemicals with other side effects. Some of these are increased risk of lung cancer, bronchitis, emphesyma, heart disease, and many other forms of cancer. It impacts other people too, as second hand smoke can cause cancer, heart disease, and other illnesses.

The brain?

Nicotine is absorbed through the blood and taken to the brain. It causes increased breathing and heart rate, while also releasing dopamine and triggering the reward center of the brain.

Treatment

Nicotine specifically is considered to be extremely addictive, as it sends dopamine to the brain. This makes it extremely difficult to quit, but there are ways to do so. A major method used is Nicotine Replacement Therapy (NRT), where products (such as gums, patches, inhalers, and lozenges) with smaller amounts of nicotine are given to people with addictions to gradually reduce their dependence on it. These also do not have the same harmful chemicals found in cigarettes, making it safer. There are many other products that also decrease cravings without the use of nicotine as well. People can also see tobacco treatment specialists to help them quit.

Street Names: Smokes, cigs, rollies, vapes, snuff, chew

Classification: Plant alkaloid considered toxic waste, highly addictive

What are the consequences?

Short-term

Some consequences are worsened mood (irratibility, restlessness, anxiety, difficulty concentrating), insomnia, and increased hunger. Additionally, many people's social lives suffer as a result of being unable to smoke in those situations.

Long-term

Long-term, it can cause lung cancer or disease, other forms of cancer, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, tooth or gum disease, and many other illnesses. These risks apply to non-smokers around them too.

Fun Facts

There are specific situations that give people a higher urge to smoke. Some of these are drinking coffee, talking on the phone, drinking alcohol, and hanging out with friends are all common triggers.
